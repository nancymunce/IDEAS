Three Dimensions of Engagement (Click to Explore)

Effective online activities engage students across three interconnected dimensions:

Behavioral Engagement: (Doing the Work)

Students actively participate and produce visible evidence of learningâ€”logging in regularly, submitting assignments, and contributing to discussions.

Design Strategies:

  • Replace passive reading with creating artifacts (concept maps, infographics, explainer slides)
  • Use structured reflection prompts like 3-2-1 (3 takeaways, 2 questions, 1 real-world connection)
  • Provide clear deliverables and rubrics that make effort purposeful

Example: Interactive Resource Hunt â€” Students find one credible source extending the module topic, summarize its key point in two sentences, and tag a peer's post that complements their own.

Emotional Engagement: (Feeling Connected)

Students feel motivated, interested, and connected to the content, instructor, and peers. They see themselves in the work and feel part of a learning community.

Design Strategies:

  • Ask for personal relevance: "How does this apply to your workplace or community?"
  • Offer choice in how students demonstrate learning (video, slides, infographic)
  • Acknowledge challenges and celebrate progress
  • Maintain clear, timely, and supportive communication

Example: "My Why" Reflection â€” Students describe why the week's concept matters to their future, then respond to a peer whose perspective resonates with them.

Cognitive Engagement: (Thinking Deeply)

Students move beyond recall and memorization into analysis, evaluation, and application. They think critically about concepts and reflect on their learning.

Design Strategies:

  • Use scenarios or case dilemmas requiring justification and comparison
  • Reward reasoning, evidence, and transfer to new contexts

Passive Examples:

  • Reading textbook chapters
  • Listening to podcasts

Active Examples:

  • Applying concepts through case studies or scenarios

Design Tip: Transform passive activities into active ones by adding a requirement to respond, create, or apply.

Three Types of Interaction

Strong engagement involves multiple forms of interaction. Click on the headings for more detail!

Learner-to-Instructor

Clear, timely, and supportive communication helps students feel connected and motivated. This includes emails, discussion replies, feedback on assignments, and video messages.

Best Practices:

  • Send weekly announcements or check-in messages
  • Participate meaningfully in discussions
  • Provide constructive, specific feedback

Learner-to-Content

Students interact with readings, videos, assignments, and quizzes through annotation, reflection, or application.

Best Practices:

  • Align all materials to learning objectives
  • Create authentic, interesting assignments
  • Use a conversational tone in instructions so content feels approachable

Learner-to-Learner

Students collaborate, respond to peers, and build community through discussions, peer reviews, and group activities.

Best Practices:

  • Design discussion prompts that encourage conversation, not just answers
  • Require substantive replies that build on peers' ideas
  • Structure peer review and group projects with clear expectations

Quick Design Check

Before launching your next module, answer these questions:

  • Does this activity: Invite students to think deeply (not just recall)?
  • Does this activity: Give me a chance to respond meaningfully?
  • Does this activity: Connect to students' real-world contexts?
  • Does this activity: Require students to do something active?

If you can answer yes to most of these, you're designing for engagement!
